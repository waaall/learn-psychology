# MBTI is not rigorous science, but it is not astrology either

- Astrology categorizes people externally by birth date and ignores many important relevant factors. For personality/character formation, the influence of birth date is probably negligible.
- MBTI at least attempts to describe psychological preferences through questionnaires and theoretical dimensions, rather than being completely detached from measurement like astrology and ignoring obvious strong correlates.
- Its problem lies in the measurement quality, stability, and theoretical foundation being relatively limited, not in the claim that it measures nothing at all.

## Understanding how MBTI categories work

- I / E (Introversion / Extraversion)
- S / N (Sensing / Intuition)
- T / F (Thinking / Feeling)
- J / P (Judging / Perceiving)

If you only rely on these letters or the popular phrases circulating online, serious distortion of meaning is inevitable. It's like saying F=ma; even if you say F is force, m is mass, and a is acceleration, that does not mean the other person truly understands classical mechanics. However, the fact that "popular understanding is often distorted" does not by itself prove that "MBTI theory is valid." We still need to evaluate it on its own merits.

To make it easier to understand, we can roughly describe mental activity with these distinctions:

- Related to oneself / related to the external world;
- More concrete / more abstract;
- More logical ("not about people") / more emotional ("about people");

Of course, you could also further divide other people into: family, friends, colleagues, supervisors... then you could have 64 personality types, right? But being able to subdivide more finely does not mean that simple classification is pseudoscience.

### I/E

If you understand it with this simplified framework, MBTI treats I/E approximately as: whether a person's attention and psychological energy more often turn inward to their own subjective world, or more often turn outward to interaction.

But this is only a helpful approximation, not the strictest MBTI definition. The controversy around MBTI is not whether people can be categorized, but whether these dimensions are measured accurately, whether they are stable, and whether they have enough explanatory power.

This "psychological energy" is easy to mistake for mysticism, but it is not mystical at all. For example: after a long day in the office, an I person can go home and lie down for a while and feel "recharged" even without scrolling their phone. But if an E person goes home and is told not to sleep and just lie there, they will get increasingly annoyed and restless. They might feel better going out to see friends. This is hard for many I people to imagine (I people usually do not feel like socializing when they are mentally tired), even if the I person can appear talkative in social situations. When they get home, they feel, finally I can rest and it feels so good.

However, there really is no systematic neuroscience theory and practice that serves as a solid foundation for this, so it feels a bit like a house built in the air, but it is not astrology.

### S/N OR T/F

These two axes are easy to confuse with T/F, because S "sensing" sounds similar to F, and N sounds like T. But they differ in many fundamental ways.

#### Official explanation

- N and S are closer to "perceiving preference."
- T and F are closer to "judging preference."

The common explanation is that N/S is not about different sensory organs, but about whether a person tends to focus on concrete facts, details, and experience when receiving and attending to information, or whether they tend toward patterns, associations, and possibilities.

T/F is also not "rational vs irrational." It means that when a person makes judgments, they tend to value consistency and logical standards, or they tend to value values, relationships, feelings, and interpersonal impact.

#### My understanding

##### First difference

A common explanation is that N and S describe how information is taken in, while T and F describe how information is processed. That explanation is too crude and easily leads to misunderstanding. Human sensory organs are largely the same, and the ways we receive information are similar—vision, hearing, touch... there may be differences, but that is not the main issue here. In fact, both are about processing information. The difference between S/N is more like a difference in the focus and organization of information.

- N and S are more like a "passive" way of processing information (preference).
- T and F are more like an "active" way of processing information (preference).

What does passive mean? I used to not understand it either.

Until AI (LLM)! An LLM has input and output, but we do not understand how the input is transformed into the output (note: I am saying we do not understand it, not that we do not know it. People working in the field certainly know that the input passes through tensor operations and nonlinear functions to produce output, but we do not understand, we do not know why this yields that output). Before LLMs, traditional algorithms were explainable; some people may not understand them, but there were always people who could. We could break the problem down step by step and solve it. That is understandable.

Yet the human brain is like a huge nonlinear LLM. Somehow we have one kind of thinking that resembles traditional algorithmic "active" operation. The typical distinction is that you can ask someone, "What do you think about MBTI?" and they can answer. This is active thinking with traceable steps that can be expressed in human language. The other kind of thinking is more like how an LLM works. Many subsystems operate in that way. For example, after seeing someone once, you can recognize them from an image. How do you recognize them? Does your brain think, "they have double eyelids, a big nose, dark skin"? No, you just recognize them! We can think of this as a specialized VLM that trains while sleeping and does inference during the day, then reports recognition results to the brain's "traditional algorithm" module for later analysis.

So from this perspective, prosopagnosia may be the result of that "VLM" module not developing well, forcing people to use traditional algorithmic strategies for face recognition. The results are predictable: humans are still not very good at it, and even the VLM models themselves do not know exactly how they recognize faces. From this perspective, ASD spectrum may involve a specialized neural system for analyzing and recognizing facial expressions and emotions that is not fully developed. High-functioning Asperger's may be a case where the CPU and traditional algorithm are strong enough to compensate, achieving recognition comparable to a specialized neural network running on a GPU.

Back to S/N: N represents that our brain tends to select, or is good at selecting, information (vision, hearing, touch) processed by an abstract specialized neural network, such as information about a person's character. S may represent a tendency to judge concrete details about a person.

But is it that we are good at it or just that we prefer it? MBTI theory does not make that clear, but often preference and skill reinforce each other.

A simple example makes it clear: after meeting a person once and having some impression, an S-leaning person will tend to remember that they have double eyelids or similar features. An N person may tend to remember their personality, such as that they are quiet, seem thoughtful but not good at expressing themselves.

In other words: people who lean toward S may more easily remember concrete details such as appearance, clothing, or speaking habits, while people who lean toward N may more easily remember an overall impression, such as the person's temperament, thinking style, or expression style.

##### Second difference

- N and S represent abstraction and concreteness. The concrete can be a person or an object, and the abstract can also be a person or an object.
- T and F represent thinking and feeling. Both are abstract, but feeling is an abstraction related to people, while thinking can be understood as an abstraction unrelated to people (treating things without treating them as people).

This shows that the previous example was not quite accurate. More precisely, under the same N preference, NF is more likely to notice a person's character, sense of relationship, and emotional state—abstract content that is related to people; NT is more likely to notice a person's reasoning, argument structure, and analytical style—abstract content that is more about ideas. Similarly, under the same S preference, SF tends to notice concrete, people-related things like tone, politeness, facial expression, and whether the other person feels comfortable; ST tends to notice concrete, task-related things like whether the person's attire is appropriate, whether their expression is clear, whether they act efficiently, and whether the details are reliable.

Combined with the two differences described above, you can see that N/S and T/F have many fundamental distinctions. Therefore, their combinations are completely different.

### P/J

P/J is also easily misunderstood as "disciplined vs procrastinating" or "plans vs does not plan," but those are also inaccurate expressions.

A more appropriate understanding is: when facing incomplete information and an undecided situation (external), does a person tend to narrow things down and decide early, or tend to stay open and continue exploring possibilities?

J tends to form judgments quickly, establish standards, and push things toward closure. That does not mean they are necessarily more efficient; it means they psychologically prefer to "settle it first."
P tends to continue observing, preserve flexibility, and delay conclusions. That does not mean they are necessarily sloppy; it means they psychologically prefer to "wait and see."

So J/P is not about ability levels, but about preferences for "certainty" versus "openness." J people can still procrastinate, and P people can still be efficient; usually the former feel worse when things remain unresolved, while the latter feel worse when things are decided too early.

### Why does I/E pair with the others?

If S/N is more like "what you tend to pay attention to," and T/F is more like "what standard or preference you tend to use to judge," then I/E is really about where those mental activities are directed.

Because for the same N, some people seem to keep seeing new possibilities in external information, while others seem to keep generating associations and an overall picture internally. The same applies to F: some people care more about external relationships and interaction atmosphere, while others care more about their own internal value feelings.

In other words, S/N and T/F only answer "which processing style do you prefer," while I/E adds "is that processing mainly directed inward or outward." That is why Ni, Ne, Si, Se, Ti, Te, Fi, Fe are eight basic "mental activity modes" (cognitive functions), and then P/J plus the balance principles below determine the priority order of each person's eight functions.

### Why balance?

The auxiliary function balance principle in type dynamics has two balances: one is the I/E balance, and the other is the S/N and T/F balance. Of course, this "balance principle" is more like a theoretical assumption and lacks a solid practical foundation.

#### I/E balance

If you are I, your dominant function is i and your auxiliary is e. Why are both not i? That might lead someone to depression.

- People need both internal reflection and external action, so dominant and auxiliary functions should include one inward and one outward.
- If you are E, that "external function" is the dominant function.
- If you are I, that "external function" is the auxiliary function, while the dominant function is the introverted version of the middle letters.

#### S/N and T/F balance

- If the dominant function is always S/N, a person will keep taking in information and find it hard to close things.
- If the dominant function is always T/F, a person will keep making judgments and find it hard to update input. Therefore the auxiliary function must complement the other type.

## Are test results unstable?

Many people reject MBTI because it is unstable and test results change. This criticism cannot be dismissed by analogy alone; it needs to be analyzed.

1. Are the test questions and scoring method reliable?
2. Does the test taker clearly understand the questions?
3. Does the test taker answer as honestly as possible?
4. More importantly: even if the state does not change much, can the same person easily cross the binary threshold when retested in a short period?

The first three issues are common to many psychological measures. Just because a tool is widely used and some people misunderstand it, and because many people use it poorly, does not make it garbage. It is like iPhone: billions of units shipped, thousands of failures do not mean it is poor quality. Many things become the subject of unfounded controversy simply because they are widely used.

But the fourth issue is precisely one of MBTI's core controversies. Because it is not a slight fluctuation of a continuous score, but sometimes a direct shift to a different type.

Even in top hospitals, many diseases have high misdiagnosis rates. Does that mean modern medicine is pseudoscience? No. On the other hand, people change. For example, resting heart rate might be 42 this year and 48 next year. Does that mean heart rate measurement is not scientific? So "changing" by itself is not enough to invalidate all psychological measurement; but "type frequently changing on retest" does weaken MBTI's credibility as a typology tool. This point needs to be acknowledged directly.

Still, there are not many psychological theories that stand up to rigorous scrutiny. The Big Five? I even think that is quite dubious.

## Does the theory hold up to testing?

Psychology is more complex than physics, but that does not mean any psychological classification tool should be treated as reliable just because it "sounds plausible." At best, MBTI can be considered a coarse-grained, debatable framework with limited evidential strength. But it is not valid to reject or accept it blindly just because one person finds it inaccurate or accurate. Traditional Chinese medicine is a typical example. Unlike more formal fields, a large part of psychology is still built on classification and induction rather than robust hypotheses and quantitative predictive models, so quantitative testing is harder to apply.

Whether it can withstand testing must be viewed dialectically. If any theory that fails practice is expelled from science, then very few theories would remain. Yet strictly speaking, outdated theories should be "driven out," such as Newtonian mechanics, and we still learn and use them extensively. Why?

Because in practice we must trade off calculation cost, ease of understanding, and predictive precision. So we typically do not use relativity or quantum mechanics to compute a pendulum experiment or a spring oscillator; when we compute satellite GPS, we use special relativity rather than only Newtonian mechanics because the error exceeds the range we need. When calculating voltage and current, we generally do not consider quantum mechanics or Maxwell's equations, even though they are clearly more correct for explaining electrons and electromagnetic fields. Therefore, we do not say "you are doing pseudoscience" when someone uses a "wrong" theory to calculate voltage or current, because practice has already shown that the theory is wrong but still useful.

Of course, these phenomena are rare, partly because physics and mathematics have high barriers that prevent a lot of people from taking sides. For example, computer science has lower barriers, so there is clear tribalism in programming language choice. In fact, choosing a computer language is analogous to choosing a theory for physical calculation: if you want the best performance and lowest latency, use C or even assembly; if you want fast development and do not care as much about performance, use TypeScript or Python; if you want a balance of stability and performance for industrial software, use Qt; if you want decent performance and stable backend development, use Java or Go. Someone who truly understands computing will not blindly take sides, but will choose the tool that best fits the project. If you learn a language only superficially and bring it into a field you are not suited for, you may turn that into tribalism to protect fragile confidence and avoid shame.

MBTI's rigor is indeed far less than physics or computer science, but using physics and computer science rigor as the standard for psychology is itself a kind of either/or tribalism. Because human understanding of brain mechanisms is severely insufficient, there is a huge theoretical gap between brain phenomena and physical structure. How can we distinguish brain phenomena? Right now, we probably have to summarize them with classification and induction. In any case, I cannot think of a better method.

### Does the theory make sense?

The section "Understanding how MBTI categories work" already shows it makes a fair amount of sense. It does capture some common differences in where people attend, how they prefer information, and how they judge.

But "sounds reasonable" and "has been strictly validated" are different things. Compared with highly formal fields like physics, mathematics, and computer science, MBTI's foundations, logical rigor, and testability are much weaker. A more appropriate attitude is neither to mythologize it nor to dismiss it as astrology, but to acknowledge that it may be inspirational while its boundaries are clear.

## What use is MBTI for oneself?

I think MBTI can have some guiding value for oneself, but only if it is treated as a heuristic tool rather than a diagnostic conclusion. First, one must understand roughly what its dimensions are saying.

In that case, you can use it as a low-cost entry point for self-observation, helping you summarize preferences, work style, and communication style, and reduce some internal friction. Can you do this without MBTI? Of course, if you understand enough details about your own preferences, habits, and patterns. MBTI only reduces the complexity of organizing these questions to some extent, and that inevitably comes with underfitting, meaning distortion.

## What use is MBTI for social interaction?

The biggest value for social interaction is that it helps you recognize that others may attend to information differently, express themselves differently, and care about different things when making decisions. These differences often come with both strengths and weaknesses.

1. Different people have different thinking styles, and that is normal.
2. Strengths and weaknesses involve trade-offs; if you cannot have everything, you should not expect others to have everything.
3. You can use this to quickly judge whether someone is compatible with your thinking style, but not as a rigid rule. Compared with judging people by appearances, this is still more reasonable.

But if you take a type and then explain everything about someone solely through that label, or use it to fix someone in a box, that will distort reality. It is meant to remind you "do not assume others are like me," not to label people.

Just as learning about ADHD, ASD, NPD, BPD, and OCPD can help me better understand myself, others, and the complex, interesting human brain, MBTI can mean something similar to me.

In the end, understanding your own preferences, weaknesses, and mental activity patterns can help you reduce unnecessary inner friction and better play to your strengths. Understanding others' points of attention, expression styles, and behavior tendencies can help reduce misunderstandings and conflict, making relationships more peaceful. If MBTI has enough accuracy to provide some inspiration in this sense, then it already has value.
